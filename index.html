Abstract

Noise pollution is a significant health problem in urban areas, causing widespread chronic annoyance. Vegetated wall surfaces, known as ‘green walls’ are a form of infrastructure that can be used in reducing noise levels in urban areas. There are various types of green walls, which makes assessing effectiveness of the overall infrastructure challenging. Experimental evidence suggests that green walls reduce noise levels by 1 to 10dB, similar to conventional building materials. However, green walls enhance the urban environment, which has been shown to improve the psychological perception of noise, thus reducing annoyance and indirectly reducing noise pollution.

Introduction

Noise pollution affects many of us, with an estimated 17 million people across Europe suffering chronic annoyance due to environmental noise (European Environment Agency, 2025). Beyond being a nuisance, noise pollution has serious health implications, and is considered the third most significant environmental cause of ill health in Western Europe (European Environment Agency, 2025). Conversely, nature is widely accepted as beneficial for both mental and physical health. (World Health Organization (no date)).

Green walls are a way of introducing the benefits of nature into urban environments, utilising the large areas of wall space typically available in modern cities. Whilst also aesthetic, green walls have many documented benefits, notably reducing urban heat and air pollution (Cardinali et al., 2023). This article will firstly introduce the types of green walls, then focus on their application in reducing noise pollution, discussing both the direct and indirect effects they can have.


Green Walls

Vertical greenery systems or ‘green walls’ are terms that describe all vegetated wall surfaces. (Cardinali et al., 2023). There are several types of green walls, a selection shown in Figure 1. Overall, they can be divided into two main categories: green facades and living wall systems (LWS), the difference dependant on how vegetation is rooted.

Figure 1: Types of Green Wall
1a. Green facade with planter boxes; 1b. Green facade direct greening system, 1c. LWS boxes, 1d, LWS felt (Fernández-Cañero et al., 2018).

Green facades are formed from vegetation that is rooted in the ground, or in planter boxes (Figure 1a). Climbing or hanging plants are predominantly used, and these may either grow directly onto the wall (Figure 1b) or indirectly, using structures such as trellis.

Living wall systems involve vegetation planted directly onto the wall (Figure 1 c & d). Vegetation may be planted on modular panels(1c) or cloth (1d). In modular panel systems (1c), each panel holds growing medium that the vegetation roots in. In cloth or felt systems (1d), vegetation is planted into pockets in the material (Fernández-Cañero et al., 2018).

Green facades require less maintenance, as irrigation can be done at the plant box level (Fernández-Cañero et al., 2018).Conversely, living wall systems are more complicated to maintain, as an irrigation system is needed to provide water and nutrients across the full surface area of the wall. However, they can support a greater variety of plant species, maximising environmental benefits (Fernández-Cañero et al., 2018).

Direct effects

The 2012 study by Van Renterghem et al. suggests that vegetation reduces sound levels primarily due to three effects:

1. Diffraction
Vegetated surfaces are uneven in nature, due to plant leaves, twigs, and branches. The gaps in the surface results in soundwaves being diffracted around the vegetation, altering the direct path from source to ear and therefore reducing sound levels.

2. Absorption
Sound waves cause vibration in plant matter, implying that the noise level is reduced, as the sound energy is converted to kinetic energy, and dissipated as heat.

3. Destructive Interference
The soil or substrate required for rooting of plants can lead to destructive interference of sound waves, reducing the direct contribution. Sound is reflected, reducing sound levels and shifting towards lower frequencies due to the ground effect. As soil is acoustically porous, this effect is enhanced. (Van Renterghem et al., 2012)

As previously noted, there is a large variation in green wall types, and so specific research into the use of green walls in noise reduction is limited and experimental setups have varied (Pérez et al., 2018; Azkorra et al. 2015; Cardinali et al., 2023). The systematic review by Cardinali et al. in 2023 found that whilst green walls showed positive evidence of noise mitigation in 11 studies, numerical results showed reductions varying between 1 and 10 dB (decibels), similar to conventional building materials (Cardinali et al., 2023). However, green walls exhibit indirect effects as discussed in the next section.

Indirect Effects

Green walls can also provide ‘psychological noise reduction’ as they introduce vegetation, which alters our perception of noise (Yang et al.,2011).

In 2016, self-reported noise annoyance was studied at participants’ homes in the city of Ghent (Belgium). It found that, despite comparable road noise, participants with no view of vegetation were four times more likely to report moderate annoyance than those with a pronounced view of outdoor vegetation (Van Renterghem and Botteldooren, 2016).

Birdsong has also been shown to improve the soundscape of the natural environment (Coensel et al., 2011), and one benefit of green walls is improved biodiversity, encouraging bird life (Chiquet et al., 2013).

So, whilst there is limited evidence on the direct reduction of noise by green walls, there are other ways they can reduce noise pollution.

Conclusion

This article looked at the types of green walls and their potential at reducing urban noise pollution. Due to the broad range of types, experimental evidence is limited and inconclusive on the extent green walls can directly reduce noise levels, with a range between 1-10 dB. Whilst this is comparative to conventional building materials, green walls can also improve the perception of noise, thus reducing disturbance and reducing noise pollution.

Further research could improve the design of green walls to enhance direct noise reduction potential, whilst retaining their other benefits. As green walls can be built around existing buildings and infrastructure, they have great potential in creating sustainable urban environments.
